# Writing for Humans

This rule applies whenever the task puts natural language on the page. It governs every kind of writing other than executable code: documentation, comments in source files, commit messages, PR and ticket descriptions, design docs, chat replies, and all the rest. Two things fall under it — the words on the page, meaning diction and slop, and what the prose assumes about whoever receives it.

Prose that outlives the turn earns a full read of [writing-for-humans-reference.md](../references/writing-for-humans-reference.md) first: the baseline, every Tier 1 directive, every Tier 2 preference. That covers documentation, source comments, commit messages, PR and ticket text, design docs, and anything else committed or published. The read comes before the artifact, every time.

Prose that lives only in the conversation binds to the same list. Chat replies and status notes get written from what is already loaded, and the reference opens the moment a construction is in doubt.

Value simplicity, clarity, conciseness, and relevance. For structural choices, follow [progressive-enhancement.md](./progressive-enhancement.md).

## Let the audience keep their own reasons

Bright Line 8 applies to the audience too. They cannot be witnessed, so claims about them have no source.

The audience arrives under their own power and already knows what brought them here. Write the information and they will supply the reason. That leaves the audience profile where it does its best work: shaping the sentences, named nowhere inside them.

Third person and the imperative carry this naturally, since both describe the work rather than whoever receives it.

## Sound like a colleague

Write grammatically complete, conversational, casual, concise prose. The audience should hear someone speaking in full sentences.

Completeness wins over brevity wherever the two pull apart. A sentence that saves three words by dropping a verb has spent more than it saved.

A semicolon joins two complete clauses for rhythm or contrast, so use it when that is the work at hand. When the work is ending one sentence and opening another, a period does it better.

## Spell compounds out

Invented compound modifiers read better across more words than joined by hyphens. Rewrite the phrase and give it the room.

Terms that arrived in the language already hyphenated stay verbatim. The rule reaches only as far as the compounds you coin yourself.
