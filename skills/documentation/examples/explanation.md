# Explanation: Why Tea Is Brewed, Not Boiled

<!-- Quadrant: Explanation. Source: https://diataxis.fr/explanation/ -->

Tea is brewed rather than boiled because the compounds that give tea its flavour, colour, and caffeine do not all behave the same way under heat, and the goal of brewing is to extract the pleasant compounds while leaving the unpleasant ones behind. This is an accident of chemistry married to centuries of cultural preference, and it shapes every rule you will read about tea.

## The chemistry, briefly

A tea leaf carries three broad groups of compounds of interest: caffeine, polyphenols (including the tannins responsible for astringency), and volatile aromatics (the smell). Caffeine extracts quickly into hot water; volatile aromatics also extract quickly but evaporate if the water is kept boiling; polyphenols extract more slowly. When you pour hot — not boiling — water onto leaves and let it sit, caffeine and aromatics come out first, followed by polyphenols. Stop the extraction at the right moment and the cup is balanced. Let it go too long and the tannins dominate, producing bitterness.

## Why culture matched the chemistry

The Chinese tradition of brewing with covered vessels, the British preference for pre-warming the pot, and the Japanese attention to water temperature for green tea are all independent discoveries of the same chemistry. Covered vessels keep aromatics from escaping. Warmed pots prevent the water from dropping below extraction temperature. Lower temperatures for green tea prevent the faster extraction of tannins from the less-oxidised leaf.

None of these traditions were designed by someone reading chemistry papers. They evolved as practitioners noticed what tasted right.

## Why a tutorial on brewing tea is not an explanation

A tutorial on brewing tea tells you *what* to do — heat the kettle, fill the pot, wait 4 minutes. An explanation tells you *why* those steps work, which lets you adapt when conditions change. If the kettle is slow, you now know why pre-warming the pot matters. If you are brewing a new tea, you know which variable to adjust.

This is the distinction Diátaxis names: tutorials and explanation serve different needs, and trying to meet both in one doc serves neither. See `examples/tutorial.md` for the tutorial counterpart.

## Implications

- Different teas warrant different temperatures because their leaves differ in oxidation level, and oxidation changes how fast polyphenols extract.
- Re-steeping the same leaves gives a different cup because the fast-extracting compounds are already gone; what remains is slower polyphenols.
- Cold brew is not a mistake; it is a slow extraction that produces a different balance of the same compounds.
